The Identity of the Restrainer

Who Is Holding Back the End? The Case for the Archangel Michael

An Accessible Study in Biblical Prophecy

Overview

The apostle Paul wrote to a church in Thessalonica that something — or someone — was currently holding back the appearance of a great evil figure at the end of history. He called this force "the restrainer." But he never gave it a name, because his readers already knew who he meant. For nearly two thousand years, Bible scholars have debated the identity of this mysterious restrainer. This study argues that a well-supported answer, drawn from the Bible itself and from ancient Jewish writings, is the archangel Michael — the same angel who appears in the book of Daniel as the guardian of God's people.

Section 1The Passage and Its Big Problem

What Paul Wrote

In his second letter to the Thessalonians, Paul addresses a church that was confused and frightened about the end times. Some people thought the final day of judgment had already arrived. Paul reassures them that before that day comes, two things must happen first: a great rebellion against God, and the appearance of a figure he calls "the man of lawlessness" — a person who will claim to be God himself.

Then Paul writes something puzzling:

"And you know what is restraining him now so that he may be revealed in his time. For the mystery of lawlessness is already at work. Only he who now restrains it will do so until he is out of the way. And then the lawless one will be revealed…" 2 Thessalonians 2:6–8 (ESV)
What does "restraining" mean?

The Greek word Paul uses is katechon (pronounced kah-TEK-on), which means "to hold back" or "to suppress." Paul is saying something — or someone — is actively preventing this evil figure from appearing before the right time. When that restrainer steps aside, the evil figure will be revealed.

The Strange Grammar Clue

Here is where it gets interesting. In verse 6, Paul calls the restrainer "what is restraining" — a thing. But in verse 7, he switches to "he who now restrains" — a person. This shift from "what" to "he" is not a mistake. It is a deliberate clue. Paul is describing both an office or principle (the "what") and the specific being who fills that role (the "he").

Any answer to the identity question must explain both: what is the restraining principle, and who is the restraining person?

The Main Candidates

Over the centuries, scholars have proposed five main answers:

  1. The Roman Empire and its emperors
  2. The preaching of the gospel throughout the world
  3. The Jewish state
  4. The Holy Spirit
  5. The archangel Michael

This study argues that option five — Michael — is a strong answer, and that it fits the clues Paul gives us well.

Section 2The Key: Daniel's Prophecy

Paul Was Drawing from Daniel

An important key to understanding the restrainer is recognizing where Paul got his ideas. His description of the "man of lawlessness" closely echoes a prophecy in the book of Daniel. Look at how the two passages parallel each other:

Daniel 11:36–37 — Written ~530 BC 2 Thessalonians 2:3–4 — Written ~AD 51
"He shall do as he wills. He shall exalt himself and magnify himself above every god, and shall speak astonishing things against the God of gods." (ESV) "The son of destruction, who opposes and exalts himself against every so-called god or object of worship, so that he takes his seat in the temple of God, proclaiming himself to be God." (ESV)

Both passages describe the same kind of figure: someone who defies all authority, exalts himself above God, and declares himself divine. Paul is building his description of the end-time evil figure directly on Daniel's ancient prophecy.

Daniel 12:1 — The Verse That Follows

This is a significant connection. Immediately after the passage Paul echoes (Daniel 11:36–37), Daniel 12:1 introduces Michael:

"At that time shall arise Michael, the great prince who has charge of your people. And there shall be a time of trouble, such as never has been since there was a nation till that time. But at that time your people shall be delivered, everyone whose name shall be found written in the book." Daniel 12:1 (ESV)
Breaking this down

Notice three things: (1) "At that time" — Michael's action is linked to the same troubled period the evil king occupies. (2) "Shall arise" — in Hebrew this word suggests active, protective action, not merely passive presence. (3) "The great prince who has charge of your people" — Michael serves as a guardian figure appointed by God to watch over his people.

The Literary Logic

Here is the key insight: when Paul borrows a description of the evil end-time figure from Daniel 11, the very next verse in Daniel introduces Michael as the one who stands guard during that exact period. It is highly likely Paul intended his readers to make that connection. The logic works like this:

Daniel 11:36–37 → Paul's "Man of Lawlessness" (2 Thess. 2:3–4)

Daniel 12:1 → Paul's "Restrainer" (2 Thess. 2:6–7)

When Paul said "you know what is restraining him," he expected his readers — who had been taught Daniel's prophecy — to recognize Michael immediately. The source text provides both the villain and the guardian in the same passage.

How Michael Fits the "What" and the "He"

Remember the grammar puzzle — why does Paul switch from "what" to "he"? Michael's role in Daniel provides the perfect explanation. The "what" refers to the heavenly restraining principle — the angelic host, or the divine office of guardian that God has established over his people. The "he" refers to Michael personally — the specific archangel who fills that role. Office and officeholder. Principle and person. Both fit Michael exactly.

Section 3What Jewish Writings Tell Us

Paul did not write in a vacuum. He lived and thought within a rich tradition of Jewish religious writing. In the centuries just before and after Jesus, Jewish scholars produced many writings that expand on the biblical picture of angels — especially Michael. These writings show us what Paul's readers would have assumed about Michael's role.

The Dead Sea Scrolls: Michael as the Prince of Light

The Dead Sea Scrolls are ancient Jewish manuscripts discovered in 1947, written by a community of devout Jews around the same time the New Testament was being written. One of these scrolls, called the War Scroll (1QM), describes a great cosmic war at the end of history. In this scroll, Michael is named the "Prince of Light" — the commander of God's angelic armies — who fights directly against the chief evil figure, called Belial.

Who is Belial?

In these ancient Jewish texts, "Belial" is the name given to the chief demonic figure — similar to how the New Testament uses "Satan" or "the devil." The Dead Sea Scrolls present Michael as the one specifically tasked with restraining Belial's power until the final battle. This is exactly what Paul describes as the restrainer's function.

The War Scroll even anticipates a period when Michael's restraint is loosened and Belial's power temporarily increases — a time of great suffering for God's people. This maps perfectly onto Paul's statement that "the mystery of lawlessness is already at work" even while the restrainer is still active.

The Book of 1 Enoch: Michael as the Cosmic Jailer

Another important ancient Jewish book is 1 Enoch, a collection of visions and prophecy. The apostle Jude actually quotes from this book in his New Testament letter (Jude 14–15), which tells us it was widely read and respected. In 1 Enoch chapters 9 and 10, Michael is given a specific command by God: to bind the leaders of the fallen angels — evil supernatural beings who corrupted the earth — and imprison them until the final judgment.

Michael's primary job description in these writings is not worship, not delivering messages, but cosmic policing — restraining evil supernatural forces that would otherwise overrun humanity. Paul's readers would have known this picture of Michael immediately.

The Testament of Dan: Michael as "Your Prince"

Another ancient Jewish text, the Testament of Dan, speaks of what will happen in the "last days." It says:

"…he will raise up unto you the Lord, even Michael your prince." Testament of Dan 6:5

The phrase "your prince" is a direct echo of Daniel 12:1. Across multiple ancient texts, Michael is consistently identified as the guardian prince of God's people — the one who stands between them and the forces of evil. This was not a fringe idea; it was a mainstream belief that Paul's audience would have taken for granted.

Section 4The Book of Revelation's Clue

Michael's War in Heaven

The Book of Revelation offers further biblical support for the Michael hypothesis. In Revelation 12:7–9, a dramatic event occurs in heaven:

"Now war arose in heaven, Michael and his angels fighting against the dragon. And the dragon and his angels fought back, but he was defeated, and there was no longer any place for them in heaven. And the great dragon was thrown down, that ancient serpent, who is called the devil and Satan — the deceiver of the whole world — he was thrown down to the earth, and his angels were thrown down with him." Revelation 12:7–9 (ESV)

What Happens Right After

Notice what follows immediately in Revelation's story. After Michael casts Satan out of heaven and down to the earth, Satan turns his full rage on humanity. He empowers a terrifying figure — the Beast of Revelation 13 — who then dominates the earth for a period of three and a half years.

The connection to Paul

Compare the two sequences side by side. In Paul (2 Thess. 2): the restrainer steps aside → the lawless one is revealed. In Revelation (ch. 12–13): Michael casts Satan to earth → the Beast is revealed and empowered. The pattern is strikingly similar. Both texts describe a moment when heavenly restraint over evil is lifted, leading into an unprecedented period of suffering and evil.

Michael Steps Aside — He Is Not Defeated

It is important to be clear about what happens in Revelation 12. Michael wins the war. Satan is thrown out of heaven — he does not escape. Michael's "stepping aside" is not a defeat; it is a deliberate, God-ordained withdrawal. Michael ceases his active restraint of Satan in the heavenly realm, allowing Satan's power to be more fully expressed on earth for the appointed end-time period. This is consistent with Paul's language: the restrainer "will do so until he is out of the way" — a voluntary stepping aside, not a forced removal.

Section 5What the Early Church Believed

The Roman Empire View

The most common view among the earliest Christians was that the restrainer was the Roman Empire and its emperors. The idea was simple: as long as Rome held order in the world, the chaos of the end times could not fully break loose. One of the earliest church fathers, Tertullian (writing around AD 200), said that Christians actually prayed for the emperor's safety — because Roman order was holding back the final catastrophe.

This view made sense at the time. But it has one major problem: Rome fell in AD 476 — and the "man of lawlessness" did not appear. The theory has to be constantly updated to make "the empire" mean whatever powerful institution exists in any given century, which shows it lacks solid grounding in the text itself.

Chrysostom's Careful Words

One of the greatest early church preachers, John Chrysostom (writing around AD 400), commented carefully on this passage. He acknowledged the Roman Empire view, but his actual words reveal uncertainty:

"There are those who say this of the grace of the Holy Spirit, but not convincingly… Many say this of the Roman Empire, and this is likely. For until that empire is dissolved, he will not come." Chrysostom, Homilies on 2 Thessalonians, Hom. 4

Notice that Chrysostom does not firmly commit to any view. He does not rule out the angelic interpretation — he simply does not elaborate on it. His hesitation is itself meaningful: even this brilliant early teacher found the passage difficult, and clearly did not think the Holy Spirit view was convincing.

The Michael View in Modern Scholarship

Serious scholarly argument for Michael as the restrainer developed significantly in the twentieth century. One of the more thorough modern cases was made by scholar Colin Nicholl in his 2004 book From Hope to Despair in Thessalonica, which examined the Danielic background, ancient Jewish texts, and Paul's eschatological framework in detail. Some recent scholars have pointed out that the Michael hypothesis has received less attention than it deserves, in part because many widely-read commentaries were shaped by a tradition that already assumed the Holy Spirit was the restrainer — and so did not examine the angelic view as carefully.

Section 6Why the Holy Spirit Doesn't Fit

The most popular view in modern evangelical Christianity — especially in America — is that the restrainer is the Holy Spirit. This view is taught in many study Bibles, Sunday school curricula, and prophecy books. But when examined carefully, it runs into serious problems.

Where Did This View Come From?

The Holy Spirit view is a relatively recent development in church history. It was popularized largely through the Scofield Reference Bible, first published in 1909, and spread widely through American evangelical culture in the twentieth century. Before that, no early church father identified the restrainer as the Holy Spirit. This alone does not make the view wrong, but it raises a fair question: if Paul taught the Thessalonians that the restrainer was the Holy Spirit, why does no record of that teaching appear in the early church?

Problem 1: The Bible Does Not Assign This Role to the Spirit

Across the Bible, the Holy Spirit is associated with specific things: convicting people of sin (John 16:8), giving new life to believers (John 3:5), empowering people for service (Acts 1:8), interceding in prayer (Romans 8:26), and marking believers as belonging to God (Ephesians 1:13). These are spiritual, personal, and relational roles.

Nowhere in Scripture is the Holy Spirit described in this kind of restraining role — holding back demonic forces or preventing the rise of an end-time evil figure. When the Bible describes that kind of cosmic restraint, it tends to involve angels. Angels hold back the four winds of destruction (Revelation 7:1). An angel chains Satan for a thousand years (Revelation 20:1–3). Michael fights the demonic "prince of Persia" (Daniel 10:13). The pattern is notable.

Problem 2: The Salvation Question

A serious theological problem with the Holy Spirit view involves what happens to salvation during the tribulation period. Many who hold this position also believe that the restrainer is removed before the worst of the end times begins. But Revelation 7:9–14 describes a large number of people — "a great multitude that no one could number, from every nation" — being saved and coming to faith during that very period.

Why this raises a difficult question

Christian traditions broadly agree that no one comes to genuine saving faith without the work of the Holy Spirit. The Westminster Confession of Faith states the Spirit "effectually calls" and "enlightens" those who come to faith. If the Spirit has been removed, what brings these tribulation believers to faith? The Holy Spirit view has difficulty answering this question from within its own framework.

Proponents of this view sometimes respond by saying it is only the Spirit's "restraining ministry" that is removed, not the Spirit himself. But that is not what the text says. Paul writes that the restrainer himself will "be out of the way" — not that one of his functions will be suspended. The text refers to the departure of a being, not the pause of an activity.

Problem 3: God Cannot Be "Taken Out of the Way"

Psalm 139:7–8 asks: "Where shall I go from your Spirit? Or where shall I flee from your presence? If I ascend to heaven, you are there! If I make my bed in Sheol, you are there!" The Holy Spirit is omnipresent — present everywhere, always. God cannot be "taken out of the way" from anything, in any literal sense. He has no location from which he can be removed.

Michael, by contrast, is a created being with a specific location and function. He can step aside. He can cease an action he has been performing. The phrase Paul uses — "until he is out of the way" — fits a personal angelic being well, and sits uneasily with an omnipresent divine person.

Problem 4: The Grammar Argument Is Not Conclusive

Supporters of the Holy Spirit view often point to the grammar: the Greek word for "spirit" (pneuma) is grammatically neuter, so the shift from "what" (neuter) to "he" (masculine) could reflect the Spirit's identity as a person. This is a reasonable observation, but it is not conclusive — because the same grammar also works for Michael. "What" = the angelic host, the principle of heavenly restraint. "He" = Michael personally. The grammar is compatible with both interpretations, so it cannot settle the question on its own.

Section 7Tough Questions, Honest Answers

The Michael hypothesis raises some fair questions. Here are the most common objections and responses to them.

"If Paul meant Michael, why didn't he just say Michael?"

Paul explicitly tells us why he did not spell it out: "Do you not remember that when I was still with you I told you these things?" (2 Thess. 2:5). The Thessalonians already knew, because Paul had taught them in person. Beyond that, Paul was writing to a community living under Roman rule. Describing angelic warfare against Roman-aligned demonic powers — and warning that divine protection would one day be withdrawn — could be dangerous in a political climate where Christians were already being persecuted. The deliberate vagueness was both practical and pastoral.

"Is Michael powerful enough to hold back global evil?"

This question misunderstands how angelic power works in the Bible. Michael does not need to be all-powerful; he needs to be assigned by an all-powerful God to a specific task. In Daniel 12:1, Michael's role is divinely commissioned — it is God's authority operating through Michael's actions. The Bible consistently shows God working through created agents: angels, prophets, apostles. Michael restraining evil is God restraining evil through Michael. The objection confuses the agent with the source of power.

"Can a neuter word really refer to an angelic host, and then shift to a person?"

Yes, this is standard Greek usage. Greek regularly uses neuter forms to describe abstract roles, principles, or collective groups before introducing the specific person who embodies them. Scholar Leon Morris put it well: "The transition from neuter to masculine is more naturally explained by a shift from the office to the officeholder than by any [Holy Spirit] theory." The grammar is consistent with — and actually favors — the Michael interpretation.

"Doesn't Revelation 12 show Michael being cast out of heaven, not stepping aside?"

No — Revelation 12:8 is clear that Satan and his angels are the ones cast out, not Michael. Michael wins the war. What happens is that Michael's ongoing restraint of Satan in the heavenly realm comes to an end — Satan is expelled to earth — and Satan then pours out his fury in the earthly realm. Michael's "stepping aside" is not a defeat; it is the final act of a heavenly war that Satan loses.

Section 8Where to Go From Here

This study has made the case for Michael, but there is much more to explore. Here are several areas that reward deeper investigation:

The Aramaic Targums

The Targums are ancient Aramaic translations and expansions of the Hebrew Bible, used in Jewish synagogues. Targum Jonathan, the Aramaic version of the prophets, amplifies Michael's role in the Daniel 12 passage significantly. Studying these texts reveals how deeply the Michael-as-guardian idea was embedded in the tradition Paul inherited.

Daniel 10 — The Angelic Princes of Nations

Daniel 10:13–21 presents a fascinating picture of angelic beings called "princes" — one for Persia, one for Greece, and Michael for Israel — who fight in the heavenly realm and directly influence what happens to nations on earth. This framework of national angelic guardians is the background to 2 Thessalonians 2. A study of Daniel 10 deepens the case that Paul's restrainer is working within this same angelic-national structure.

The Ascension of Isaiah

This ancient Jewish and early Christian text describes an angel who governs the lower heavens and is displaced at the end of the age. Examining this text alongside 2 Thessalonians 2 shows that the idea of an angelic figure being "removed" to allow end-time evil to flourish was a recognizable concept in the world Paul and his readers inhabited.

How Interpretations Change Over Time

It is worth studying why the Holy Spirit view became dominant in the twentieth century — specifically how dispensationalist theology shaped popular Bible study in America. Understanding the history of an interpretation helps us read the text more clearly, free from assumptions we did not know we were bringing.

ConclusionPulling It All Together

The question Paul leaves open — who or what is restraining the final evil? — is answered best by the archangel Michael. Here is why each line of evidence points in that direction:

  • The Daniel connection: Paul's description of the evil end-time figure echoes Daniel 11:36–37. Daniel 12:1, which follows immediately, introduces Michael as the guardian who stands during that same troubled period.
  • The grammar: The shift from "what" (neuter) to "he" (masculine) is consistent with moving from the angelic office of heavenly restraint to the personal archangel who fills that role.
  • Ancient Jewish writings: The War Scroll, 1 Enoch, and the Testament of Dan describe Michael as a figure who restrains cosmic evil — a function that aligns with what Paul describes.
  • Revelation 12: Michael's casting of Satan to earth is followed by the Beast's rise — a sequence that parallels what Paul describes when the restrainer steps aside.
  • The Holy Spirit problems: The Holy Spirit view lacks clear biblical precedent for this specific function, raises difficult questions about salvation during the tribulation, and sits uneasily with the Spirit's omnipresence.

We cannot be certain — Paul assumed knowledge his original readers had that we do not. But when we follow the threads of evidence, they point toward Michael, the great prince, standing guard over God's people, and one day — at the appointed time — stepping aside.

That moment will not be a surprise to God. It is written into the plan. And the story does not end with the restrainer stepping back — it ends with the Lord himself arriving to destroy the lawless one "by the breath of his mouth" (2 Thess. 2:8). The restrainer makes way for the King.
